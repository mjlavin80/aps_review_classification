THE VICTORS

Eisenhower and His Boys:

The Men of World War II.

By Stephen E. Ambrose.

Illustrated. 396 pp. New York:

Simon & Schuster. $28.

Stephen E. Ambrose's latest book follows the World War II Allied campaign in Europe from the appointment of Gen. Dwight D. Eisenhower as commander of the European Theater of Operations to the final surrender of Germany, seven days after Hitler's suicide. Ambrose fans will have a distinct sense of deja vu; ''The Victors'' is largely composed of huge blocks of text from five of Ambrose's previous histories: ''Eisenhower,'' ''Pegasus Bridge,'' ''Band of Brothers,'' ''D-Day'' and ''Citizen Soldiers.'' Still, there is a lot to be said for having it all under one cover, for combining Ambrose's compelling portrait of Eisenhower's leadership with the vivid experiences of the infantrymen, and following the campaign from conception to conclusion.

A bigger obstacle for me is that although I am only 10 years younger than Ambrose, we are of separate generations. While he was teaching, writing and researching military history, I was leading a platoon in Vietnam. Like many in my generation, I felt betrayed by what had once seemed heroic. It has been painful for me to read about war ever since. I am always horrified, and never inspired. But then those of us who served in Vietnam are notorious for our cynicism, our distrust of authority. His was the good war, ours the bad one, and I read ''The Victors'' with the hope of narrowing what became known as the generation gap.

Ambrose starts with Eisenhower, which narrows the gap for me. Almost everyone loved Eisenhower, and he firmly set the standards for leadership. Ambrose quotes a remark Eisenhower made on the subject of pacifism -- ''I doubt whether any of these people, with their academic or dogmatic hatred of war, detest it as much as I do'' -- and when Eisenhower tells young lieutenants they must be a father for their men it strikes a hauntingly familiar chord. The quality of leadership, echoed down through the ranks, is a central theme. So are unity of purpose and the unit cohesion that results.

While I don't envy another man's war, we didn't have that in Vietnam, and the lack of it led to poor morale, occasional atrocities and defeat. So I can readily relate to these themes of leadership. I also recognize the intimate details of the infantryman's experience: digging in, using the terrain, firing and maneuvering. I am fascinated by the similarity of tactics, the characteristically American reliance on heavy firepower if the infantrymen can be spared; even the logistics of preparation can be mesmerizing, as they are in real life. But then the invasion is launched and men begin to die, first by the tens, then by the hundreds and thousands. As is always true, nobody is really prepared for the enormity of war, and no text can fully capture it. Here the generation gap begins to widen again.

Ambrose is a superb historian. He has the details and he has the voice. When, however, he writes about the citizen soldier, he himself all too often sounds more like the professional soldier. He has an admirable Eisenhower-like loyalty toward the men and a soldier's focus on the objective ahead. He assembles a huge array of details to support his mission, drawing heavily from interviews and unit histories. But in the compression of thousands of pages from his earlier books into just under 400 here, many illuminating and insightful details have been lost and jingoism comes more to the fore. The highest praise he can deliver to one of his heroes is to compare him to Sergeant York, and he does it twice. But war brings out the worst in people as well as the best, and behavior that is considered heroic in war would often be considered sociopathic if not totally insane in another setting. When Ambrose allows the men to speak for themselves, they often express very human feelings: terror, disgust and shame as well as the flush of victory. ''Whatever you do in this book, don't go making me into a bloody hero,'' a British sergeant he is interviewing says. But then Ambrose replies: ''Sergeant Thornton, I don't make heroes. I only write about them.'' Was he really listening?

Ambrose has said elsewhere that war is a political act, and his accounts here superficially avoid the politics of war, even as they deliver the message that there is something innately superior about the soldiers of a democracy. This is a familiar theme for me as well, because this is what we, too, were told as young officers. It may be true, but surely all armies send similar messages to their troops. These are messages that can lead societies toward pretty serious misadventures when politicians are added to the brew. Ambrose's projection of such a militaristic view thins his book's air of authenticity. I'm afraid that at such moments he contributes less to the history of war than to the culture of it.
